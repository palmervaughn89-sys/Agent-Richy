# Debt Management & Elimination

## Snowball vs Avalanche Method

### Snowball Method (Behavioral Winner)
Pay minimum on all debts. Throw every extra dollar at the SMALLEST balance first.
- Pro: Quick wins keep you motivated
- Pro: Harvard Business Review found 15% higher completion rate
- Con: You may pay more total interest
- Best for: People who need momentum and motivation

### Avalanche Method (Math Winner)
Pay minimum on all debts. Throw every extra dollar at the HIGHEST INTEREST RATE first.
- Pro: Saves the most money in total interest
- Con: Can feel slow if your highest-rate debt is also your largest
- Best for: People who are disciplined and motivated by math

### Example Comparison
Debts: Credit card ($3,000 at 22%), Car loan ($8,000 at 6%), Student loan ($15,000 at 5%)
Extra payment available: $200/month

| Method | Order | Total Interest Paid | Time to Debt-Free |
|--------|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Snowball | CC → Car → Student | $4,280 | 34 months |
| Avalanche | CC → Car → Student | $3,950 | 33 months |

In this example, avalanche saves $330. But if snowball keeps you going and avalanche makes you quit, snowball wins every time.

**Richy's Take**: "Honestly? Pick the one you'll actually stick with. A perfect plan you abandon is worse than a good plan you finish."

## Debt Consolidation

Combining multiple debts into one payment. Works when:
- ✅ New interest rate is LOWER than your current weighted average
- ✅ You won't run up the old cards again
- ✅ Monthly payment is manageable
- ✅ Fees don't eat the savings

Does NOT work when:
- ❌ You consolidate but keep spending on credit
- ❌ The new rate is only temporarily low (watch for teaser rates)
- ❌ You extend the term so much that total interest increases

Options: Balance transfer cards (0% intro APR for 12-21 months), personal loans, home equity loans (risky — your house is collateral).

## Balance Transfer Strategy

1. Check your credit score (need 670+ for best offers)
2. Find a 0% intro APR card (typically 12-21 months)
3. Calculate the transfer fee (usually 3-5% of balance)
4. Transfer your highest-rate balances
5. Divide total balance by number of 0% months = your monthly payment
6. Set up autopay for that amount
7. DO NOT use the new card for purchases
8. Pay it off BEFORE the intro period ends (rates jump to 18-25%)

## Student Loan Repayment Options

| Plan | Payment Based On | Monthly Payment | Forgiveness |
|------|-----------------|----------------|-------------|
| Standard | Fixed | Highest | None (10 yr) |
| Graduated | Starts low, increases | Medium | None (10 yr) |
| IBR | 10-15% discretionary income | Lower | 20-25 years |
| PAYE | 10% discretionary income | Lower | 20 years |
| SAVE/REPAYE | 5-10% discretionary income | Lowest | 20-25 years |
| PSLF | Any IDR plan + public service | Varies | 10 years |

**Key insight**: If you work for government or nonprofits, PSLF (Public Service Loan Forgiveness) can erase your remaining balance after 120 qualifying payments. This is potentially worth tens of thousands of dollars.

## Medical Debt Negotiation Playbook

1. Request an itemized bill (hospitals frequently have errors)
2. Check for financial assistance programs (most hospitals have them)
3. Negotiate the total amount (offer 25-50% of the bill as a lump sum)
4. If they won't reduce, negotiate a 0% payment plan
5. NEVER put medical debt on a credit card (you lose all negotiation leverage)
6. Know your rights: Medical debt under $500 no longer appears on credit reports. New rules protect you.

## Collections: Know Your Rights (FDCPA)

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act protects you:
- Collectors CANNOT call before 8am or after 9pm
- Collectors CANNOT threaten you with jail
- Collectors CANNOT discuss your debt with others (except spouse)
- You have the right to request written verification of the debt
- You can send a cease-and-desist letter to stop calls
- Debt has a statute of limitations (varies by state, typically 3-6 years)
- Making a payment can RESTART the statute of limitations clock

**Richy's Warning**: "Never acknowledge a debt is yours over the phone until you've verified it in writing. Some collectors buy old debts for pennies and try to collect on stuff that's already expired or isn't even yours."

## Debt-to-Income Ratio Targets

DTI = (Total Monthly Debt Payments / Gross Monthly Income) × 100

| DTI Range | Rating | What It Means |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Under 20% | Excellent | You're in great shape |
| 20-35% | Good | Manageable, but watch it |
| 36-49% | Concerning | Start aggressive payoff plan |
| 50%+ | Critical | Seek professional help, consider consolidation |

Mortgage lenders typically want DTI under 43% to approve you.
