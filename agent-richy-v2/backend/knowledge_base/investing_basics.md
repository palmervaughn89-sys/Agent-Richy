# Investing Basics

## What Is Investing?
Investing means putting your money to work so it grows over time. Instead of keeping cash under your mattress (where inflation eats its value), you buy assets — stocks, bonds, real estate, or funds — that historically increase in value.

## Key Concepts

### Stocks
A stock is a tiny ownership stake in a company. When the company profits, your share becomes worth more. Historically, the U.S. stock market returns about 10% per year on average (before inflation). Individual years can range from -37% to +52%.

### Bonds
A bond is a loan you make to a government or corporation. They pay you fixed interest and return your principal at maturity. Bonds are less volatile than stocks but offer lower returns (typically 4-6%).

### Index Funds
An index fund tracks an entire market. The S&P 500 index fund owns all 500 of the largest U.S. companies. Index funds charge very low fees (0.03-0.20%) and outperform 90% of professional fund managers over 15+ years.

### ETFs vs Mutual Funds
ETFs trade like stocks throughout the day. Mutual funds trade once per day at market close. ETFs typically have lower expense ratios and better tax efficiency. Both are excellent vehicles for diversified investing.

## Getting Started

### 1. Open a Brokerage Account
Top brokerages for beginners: Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Vanguard. All offer zero-commission trades and no account minimums.

### 2. Start with Index Funds
- **VTI** or **VTSAX** — Total U.S. stock market
- **VOO** or **FXAIX** — S&P 500
- **VXUS** — International stocks
- **BND** — Total bond market

### 3. Dollar-Cost Average
Invest the same amount on a regular schedule regardless of market conditions. This removes emotion and reduces timing risk.

### 4. Diversify
Spread investments across asset classes, sectors, and geographies. A simple 3-fund portfolio (U.S. stocks + international stocks + bonds) covers the entire global market.

## Risk and Return
Higher potential returns always come with higher risk. Stocks return more than bonds but are more volatile. Your risk tolerance depends on your time horizon — money needed in 1-3 years belongs in a savings account, not the stock market.

## Compound Growth
$10,000 invested at 10% annual return:
- Year 10: $25,937
- Year 20: $67,275
- Year 30: $174,494

Adding $500/month:
- Year 10: $127,809
- Year 20: $386,968
- Year 30: $1,085,661

Time is the most powerful factor. Starting 10 years earlier matters more than doubling your monthly contribution.
