# Financial Literacy for Kids and Teens

## Ages 3-5: Money Is Real

### Key Lessons
- Coins and bills have different values
- Things cost money — you can't just take things from stores
- Sometimes you have to wait to get what you want (delayed gratification)

### Activities
- Play store with real coins
- Let them hand money to cashier
- "Do you want this small toy now, or save for the bigger one?"

## Ages 6-10: Earning, Saving, Spending

### Key Lessons
- Money comes from work (not ATMs or phones)
- Three jars: Save, Spend, Share
- Needs vs Wants (food = need, toy = want)
- Setting savings goals

### Activities
- Earn money through age-appropriate chores
- Use clear jars so they SEE money growing
- Match their savings (Bank of Mom/Dad pays interest)
- Let them make spending mistakes with their own money — priceless learning

### Allowance Tips
- Tie some money to chores, give some unconditionally
- Start with age-appropriate amount ($0.50-$1 per year of age per week)
- Don't bail them out when they spend it all early

## Ages 11-14: Compound Interest and Entrepreneurship

### Key Lessons
- How compound interest works (use concrete examples)
- Basics of budgeting (income - expenses = savings)
- How banks work
- Inflation: why $10 today ≠ $10 in 20 years
- Introduction to entrepreneurship

### Activities
- Open a youth savings account (many banks allow with parent)
- Teach them to track spending for one month
- Start a micro-business: lawn care, pet sitting, crafts, tutoring
- Play stock market simulator games
- Show them YOUR budget (age-appropriate transparency)

### The Compound Interest Story
If a 12-year-old invests $50/month starting at 15 until age 65:
- Total invested: $30,000
- Value at 65 (8% return): ~$349,000
If they wait until 25: $30,000 invested → ~$149,000 (less than half!)

## Ages 15-18: Real World Prep

### Key Lessons
- How taxes work (they'll see it on first paycheck!)
- Credit and credit scores (NOT debt = normal)
- Compound interest on debt (credit cards)
- Basic investing (index funds, not day trading)
- Needs vs wants at a higher level (new car vs used)
- How student loans work

### Activities
- Get a part-time job
- Open a Roth IRA (if they have earned income) — best head start ever
- Get a secured credit card and learn responsible use
- Review college cost calculators
- Practice negotiating (cell phone bill, subscription prices)
- Create a personal budget

### First Paycheck Lesson
Show them the difference between gross and net pay. Explain each deduction:
- Federal income tax
- State income tax (if applicable)
- Social Security (6.2%)
- Medicare (1.45%)

## Money Conversations to Have at Every Age
1. "In our family, we choose to spend on X because Y" (values-based spending)
2. "We can't afford that right now" → Better: "That's not in our plan right now"
3. "What do you think that costs?" (develop price awareness)
4. "How could you earn money for that?" (entrepreneurial thinking)
5. "What would happen if you saved that instead?" (opportunity cost)

## Common Mistakes Parents Make
1. Never talking about money (kids learn silence = shame)
2. Always saying yes (kids don't learn delayed gratification)
3. Not letting kids fail with money (small failures now prevent big failures later)
4. Teaching fear of money instead of respect for it
5. Not modeling good behavior (kids watch what you DO, not what you say)
