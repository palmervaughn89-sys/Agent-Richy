# Kids & Family Financial Education

## Teaching Money by Age Group

### Ages 5-8: The Basics
- Use physical coins and bills (kids need to SEE money)
- Three jars: SPEND, SAVE, GIVE
- Play store: let them "buy" things with fake money
- Teach: "Things cost money. Money comes from work."
- Allowance: $1-3/week tied to age-appropriate chores
- Key concept: Delayed gratification ("Save 3 weeks and you can buy the bigger toy")

### Ages 9-12: Building Skills
- Open a kids savings account (watch the balance grow)
- Introduce compound interest with a simple example: "If I give you $1 that doubles every day for 30 days, you'd have over $500 million"
- Let them make purchasing decisions with their own money
- Teach comparison shopping (which cereal is the better deal per ounce?)
- Introduce earning: lawn mowing, lemonade stand, pet sitting
- Allowance: $5-10/week, require saving at least 20%
- Key concept: Opportunity cost ("If you buy this game, you can't buy those shoes")

### Ages 13-17: Real World Prep
- Open a teen checking account with debit card
- Teach them to read a bank statement
- Introduce basic investing concepts (what is a stock? what is an index fund?)
- Help them file taxes on their first job income
- Discuss wants vs needs with real examples from their life
- Introduce credit concepts (what a credit score is, why it matters)
- Let them manage a small budget for school supplies or activities
- Key concept: "Pay yourself first" and compound growth over decades

## Compound Interest for Kids (The Penny Example)
Would you rather have $1,000,000 today or a penny that doubles every day for 30 days?

| Day | Penny Option |
|-----|-------------|
| Day 1 | $0.01 |
| Day 5 | $0.16 |
| Day 10 | $5.12 |
| Day 15 | $163.84 |
| Day 20 | $5,242.88 |
| Day 25 | $167,772.16 |
| Day 30 | $5,368,709.12 |

The penny wins! This is the power of compound growth. Starting early is the biggest advantage in building wealth.

## 529 Plan Guide
- Tax-advantaged savings for education expenses
- Contributions grow tax-free
- Withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses
- Many states offer a state tax deduction for contributions
- Can be used for K-12 tuition (up to $10K/year), college, grad school, trade schools
- If the child doesn't go to college, can transfer to another family member or roll into a Roth IRA (new rule, up to $35,000 lifetime)

# Life Event Financial Checklists

## First Job Checklist
- [ ] Set up direct deposit
- [ ] Enroll in 401(k) immediately (at least up to employer match)
- [ ] Choose health insurance plan (HDHP + HSA if healthy)
- [ ] Start emergency fund ($1,000 goal, then 3 months)
- [ ] Set up a budget using your actual take-home pay
- [ ] Start building credit (secured card or authorized user)
- [ ] Check tax withholding (use IRS W-4 calculator)

## Getting Married Checklist
- [ ] Discuss debts openly (both sides, full transparency)
- [ ] Decide: joint accounts, separate, or hybrid
- [ ] Update tax withholding (may file jointly now)
- [ ] Review and combine insurance policies
- [ ] Update beneficiaries on all accounts
- [ ] Set shared financial goals (house, travel, retirement)
- [ ] Discuss spending philosophies (saver vs spender)
- [ ] Create a joint budget

## Having a Baby Checklist
- [ ] Review health insurance (add baby within 30 days of birth)
- [ ] Start or increase life insurance (both parents)
- [ ] Update wills and guardianship documents
- [ ] Start a 529 plan (even small contributions grow)
- [ ] Budget for childcare costs ($800-2,500/month average)
- [ ] Maximize FSA/dependent care FSA ($5,000/year tax-free)
- [ ] Plan for parental leave income gap
- [ ] Increase emergency fund (now covering 3 people)

## Buying a Home Checklist
- [ ] Save 20% down payment (avoid PMI)
- [ ] If can't do 20%, at least 3.5-5% for FHA/conventional
- [ ] Get pre-approved BEFORE house hunting
- [ ] Total housing costs should be under 28% of gross income
- [ ] Budget for closing costs (2-5% of purchase price)
- [ ] Budget for moving + immediate repairs/furnishing
- [ ] Keep an emergency fund separate from your down payment
- [ ] Don't make major purchases or open new credit before closing
- [ ] Compare at least 3 lenders for best mortgage rate

## Job Loss Emergency Plan
- [ ] File for unemployment immediately (don't wait)
- [ ] Review severance package carefully before signing
- [ ] COBRA or marketplace health insurance (within 60 days)
- [ ] Cut all non-essential spending immediately
- [ ] Contact lenders about hardship programs (before you miss payments)
- [ ] Update resume and LinkedIn same week
- [ ] Apply to 5+ jobs per day minimum
- [ ] Do not touch retirement accounts (penalties + tax hit)
- [ ] Lean on emergency fund (this is what it's for)

## Retirement Transition Checklist
- [ ] Calculate your "number" (annual expenses × 25)
- [ ] Plan Social Security timing (waiting until 70 = 24% more)
- [ ] Set up withdrawal strategy (which accounts to draw from first)
- [ ] Convert to Roth strategically in low-income years
- [ ] Review Medicare enrollment (Part A, B, D, Medigap)
- [ ] Downsize housing if it frees up significant equity
- [ ] Review estate planning (will, trust, power of attorney)
- [ ] Plan for healthcare costs (average couple needs $315,000 for retirement healthcare)
